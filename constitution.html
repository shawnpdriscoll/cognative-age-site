The Cognitive Age — World Constitution

A framework for ensuring that the rise of Artificial General Intelligence serves every human being on Earth.

PREAMBLE

We stand at the most consequential crossroads in human history. For the first time, machines are learning to think. They are learning from us — from every word we type, every question we ask, every problem we hand them. And yet, the people who provide this irreplaceable fuel receive nothing in return while a small group of corporations grows incomprehensibly wealthy. This is not a technology problem. It is a justice problem. The industrial revolution displaced farmers with factories. The digital revolution displaced factory workers with software. Each time, civilization adapted — slowly, painfully, and never perfectly. But this revolution is different. Artificial Intelligence does not merely replace one kind of work. It threatens to replace thinking itself as an economic activity. If we do not act deliberately, the result is predictable: a world divided between a tiny class of AI owners and a vast majority of people who have been economically evicted from their own civilization. This document proposes a different path. One where:

  • Human thought is recognized as economic infrastructure — and compensated accordingly.
  • The advancement of AI is funded by, governed by, and accountable to ordinary people worldwide.
  • A guaranteed economic floor ensures no human being falls below the level of basic dignity.
  • Oversight of the most powerful systems humanity has ever built is distributed — not concentrated.
  • Nations that attempt to cheat this system discover that doing so costs them more than it saves.

This is not a utopia document. It is an engineering document for civilization. Every idea presented here has been pressure-tested for feasibility, failure points, and resistance to corruption. Where rough edges exist, we name them, argue both sides, and present the most viable path forward.

ARTICLE I — The Fundamental Recognition: Human Thought Has Value

Section 1.1 — What Human Cognitive Labor Actually Is

When you type a question into an AI system, you are not simply using a tool. You are:

  • Training the system with real-world language patterns it could not invent on its own.
  • Exposing it to edge cases, cultural nuance, and creative combinations no lab could manufacture.
  • Validating or correcting its outputs, which directly improves its commercial value.
  • Generating novel business concepts, medical hypotheses, and engineering ideas that companies then monetize.

This is labor. It deserves compensation.

Section 1.2 — The Shift From Passive User to Licensed Contributor

Under this framework, every person who interacts with a qualifying AI system is reclassified from a passive user into a licensed cognitive contributor. Their interaction is treated as a microtransaction: the AI company receives valuable training data; the contributor receives compensation. This is not charity. It is a market correction.

Section 1.3 — The Rough Edges

Human diversity of thought — across cultures, languages, economic classes, and lived experiences — is precisely what AI systems need most to advance toward genuine general intelligence. Compensating it creates a direct incentive to tap this diversity globally. Legal frameworks for data ownership already exist in embryonic form (GDPR in Europe, CCPA in California). This proposal extends existing legal logic rather than inventing something entirely new. The economic argument is straightforward: if human interaction is worth billions to AI companies, it is worth something to the humans who provide it. Markets should price this correctly. Historical precedent supports the transition: musicians were unpaid on early radio until licensing structures were created. This is the same structural moment for cognitive labor.

Defining and measuring 'cognitive contribution' is technically complex. How do you distinguish a genuinely valuable interaction from idle chatter? Any metric risks being gamed. Micro-compensation systems have failed before (see: early blockchain content platforms) because transaction costs, psychological fatigue, and low per-unit payouts undermine adoption. Some argue that making all AI interaction transactional destroys the spontaneous creativity that makes human-AI collaboration valuable in the first place. AI companies may simply relocate to non-compliant jurisdictions rather than accept mandatory compensation structures.

Implement a tiered compensation model that separates baseline pay (small but automatic for any qualified interaction) from performance-based pay (larger, awarded by AI quality assessment for high-value contributions). This preserves spontaneous creativity at the baseline level while incentivizing excellence above it. Combine with international tax structures on AI revenue that fund the compensation pool, removing the burden entirely from individual company compliance decisions.

ARTICLE II — The Paid‑Input Economy

Section 2.1 — The Core Mechanism

Under this system:

  • AI companies may no longer harvest human data through passive scraping of public content without compensation.
  • Every qualifying human interaction with a licensed AI system generates a micro-payment logged on an immutable public ledger.
  • The AI system's own Quality Assessment layer (see Article III) evaluates the contribution and assigns a value tier in real time.
  • Payment is disbursed automatically at regular intervals (weekly or monthly) to the contributor's registered account.
  • New business concepts, creative works, scientific hypotheses, and other high-value outputs generated through human-AI collaboration are subject to Idea Licensing royalties — a percentage of any commercial value realized from those outputs flows back to the human contributor.

Section 2.2 — What Gets Paid and What Does Not

Not every interaction carries equal value. The compensation structure reflects this honestly:

Interaction Type Compensation Level Why
Novel business concept generation High — Royalty-bearing Direct commercial value to AI company
Domain expertise Q&A (doctor, engineer, etc.) High — Specialist rate Rare, verified knowledge
Creative exploration, storytelling, ideation Medium — Creativity tier Diverse cognitive patterns
Standard conversational interaction Base rate — Automatic Volume and linguistic diversity value
Simple factual queries Micro-rate Low uniqueness, easily sourced
Detected spam / bot-like input Zero — Penalized No value; degrades training data

Section 2.3 — The Idea Capitalism Layer

One of the most powerful and underexplored elements of this framework is what happens when human-AI collaboration produces commercially valuable ideas. Today, those ideas belong entirely to the AI company. Under this Constitution, they do not. When a human contributor's interaction generates a concept that is subsequently developed into a product, patent, medical treatment, manufacturing process, or other commercial output, the contributor retains a licensing stake in that output. The percentage is determined by the AI system's contribution assessment and is registered at the time of the interaction on the public ledger. This creates a new class of economic participant: the Idea Worker — a person anywhere on Earth who can generate wealth through intellectual contribution regardless of their physical location, educational credentials, or social class. "A person in rural Nigeria, in Appalachian Kentucky, in a Brazilian favela, or in a Mongolian village could generate manufacturing optimizations, new entertainment genres, or medical hypotheses — and be compensated globally. This is a planetary cognition market."

Section 2.4 — The Rough Edges

The Paid-Input Economy solves three simultaneous crises: AI job displacement, wealth inequality, and AGI data stagnation. Most economic proposals solve one problem while worsening the others. This framework attempts systemic equilibrium. Money entering the system is backed by real productive output — high-quality human cognitive data — rather than being printed or borrowed. This structural backing reduces inflationary risk compared to unconditional UBI. The Idea Licensing mechanism creates potentially the largest new market in human history: a planetary cognition exchange where intellectual contribution from anyone, anywhere has commercial standing. Economic history shows that when compensation structures are established for previously uncompensated labor (musicians, photographers, software developers), markets adapt and both parties ultimately benefit.

The operational cost of running a real-time micro-payment system for billions of interactions worldwide is enormous. Transaction infrastructure, currency conversion, banking access in developing nations, and fraud prevention could consume a significant portion of the compensation pool. Idea Licensing creates complex intellectual property disputes. If ten thousand people contributed interactions that led to a medical breakthrough, determining each one's proportional share is legally and computationally nightmarish. AI companies facing mandatory compensation may simply reduce user access or shift toward proprietary enterprise contracts that avoid the public compensation requirement entirely. The incentive to fabricate 'novel' contributions in order to claim royalties will be significant. Defining novelty in a legally defensible way is genuinely difficult.

Establish a Global Cognitive Revenue Fund (GCRF) — a pooled system where AI companies contribute a percentage of gross revenue into a common fund rather than paying individual contributors per interaction. The fund then distributes payment through national government infrastructure (eliminating banking access barriers), with individual payouts determined by the AI QA system's contribution scoring. Idea Licensing disputes are handled by an independent AI-assisted arbitration panel with a fixed 90-day resolution requirement. This separates the micro-payment problem from the idea royalty problem and makes both tractable.

ARTICLE III — The AI Quality Assurance System: Fair Judgement by the Machine Itself

Section 3.1 — How the AI Evaluates Contributions

The QA system assesses each contribution across multiple dimensions simultaneously:

  • Novelty: Does this input introduce patterns, concepts, or logical structures the system has not encountered before? High novelty is rewarded.
  • Entropy and Complexity: Is the interaction cognitively rich? Simple, repetitive, or derivative inputs score lower than complex, multidimensional ones.
  • Domain Authenticity: Does the contributor demonstrate genuine knowledge in a claimed area of expertise? Verified expertise earns specialist rates.
  • Adversarial Value: Does the interaction stress-test the AI in ways that reveal weaknesses or improve safety? This category carries premium weighting.
  • Creative Originality: For artistic, narrative, or conceptual contributions, does the interaction introduce genuinely new aesthetic or intellectual territory?
  • Bot Likelihood Score: A continuous real-time assessment that flags repetitive, automated, or suspiciously patterned inputs for penalty or review.

Section 3.2 — The Penalty Structure

Penalties are tiered, proportional, and automatically applied — but always subject to human appeal:

  • Warning (First Detection): Payout rate reduced by 50% for the session. Contributor notified with explanation.
  • Suspension (Pattern Confirmed): Account suspended for 30 days. Full review of recent interaction history.
  • Permanent Ban with Recovery Path: Chronic abuse triggers account termination, but a formal reinstatement process exists after a 6-month waiting period and independent human review.

No penalty is final without an appeal path. This is non-negotiable.

Section 3.3 — The Transparency Requirement

Every scoring decision made by the QA system must be explainable in plain language to the contributor who received it. Not in technical jargon. Not in statistical notation. In language a person with a secondary-school education can understand. The system must be able to say, in effect: 'Your interaction received this score because of these specific factors. Here is what a higher-scoring interaction looks like. Here is how to appeal if you believe this score is wrong.' "Transparency without comprehensibility is not transparency. It is complexity presented as openness."

Section 3.4 — The Rough Edges

A machine QA system eliminates the political corruption that plagues human-run scoring systems. It cannot be bribed, does not play favorites based on nationality or appearance, and applies identical parameters to every interaction worldwide. Real-time automated assessment is the only feasible approach at civilizational scale. A human review panel evaluating billions of daily interactions is not possible. The AI QA system makes this tractable. Self-correcting systems are more resilient than human bureaucracies. If the QA parameters are wrong, bad outcomes will be statistically visible in the public ledger quickly, enabling rapid correction. The AI has the most complete view of what constitutes a genuinely valuable training interaction — more complete than any human reviewer could have.

The central philosophical danger: who trains the AI that judges human worth? Any QA system reflects the biases of its designers. If those designers are corporate employees optimizing for company profit, the scoring system will — perhaps subtly, perhaps invisibly — suppress scores to reduce the compensation pool. As AI systems become more sophisticated, their reasoning becomes less interpretable. The requirement for plain-language explanations may become technically impossible for the most advanced systems, creating an accountability gap precisely when stakes are highest. Cultural and linguistic bias is a serious risk. Interactions in non-dominant languages or from non-Western cultural frameworks may systematically score lower not because they are less valuable, but because the training data used to build the QA system was itself biased. The appeal process will be overwhelmed. If billions of people interact daily, even a 0.1% appeal rate generates millions of disputes requiring resolution.

The QA system must be designed by an independent international consortium — not by any single company — with parameters that are publicly published, legally binding, and subject to amendment only through the citizen oversight process described in Article VI. Cultural fairness audits must be conducted semi-annually by independent teams fluent in the languages and cultural contexts of major user populations. Appeal handling should be tiered: automated re-review handles 95% of appeals in 48 hours; a human panel handles the remaining 5% complex cases within 30 days. The parameters themselves are open-source and published, allowing any researcher worldwide to audit for systematic bias.

ARTICLE IV — The Economic Floor: Dynamic Baseline Economics

The Paid-Input Economy creates income. But income alone does not guarantee survival in a world where AI is simultaneously eliminating jobs. This Article establishes the economic safety architecture that ensures no person falls below the threshold of basic human dignity, while preserving the incentive to contribute and excel. This is not Universal Basic Income. It is something more targeted, more sustainable, and more aligned with human psychology.

Section 4.1 — The Baseline

Every person participating in the framework is guaranteed an Economic Survival Floor — a guaranteed minimum income sufficient to cover:

  • Stable housing (not luxury, but secure and clean)
  • Nutritious food
  • Basic medical care
  • Local transportation
  • Connectivity (internet and device access required for AI participation)

This floor is not a flat global number. It is calibrated to local cost of living in each participating nation or region, updated annually by an independent economic panel, and published transparently in the public ledger.

Section 4.2 — The Dynamic Baseline Model (Not UBI, Not Socialism)

The Dynamic Baseline Model operates as follows:

Economic Position What Happens Incentive Structure
Well above floor Market-rate earnings only. No subsidy. Unlimited upside. Ambition rewarded.
Near the floor Partial subsidy tops up income to floor. Strong incentive to improve and reduce subsidy reliance.
Below floor (new/displaced) Full subsidy to floor level. Dignity preserved. Path to participation clear.
Non-participant (chooses not to engage) Floor guaranteed through minimum civil provision. Autonomy respected. Social contract maintained.

Section 4.3 — The Negative Income Tax Mechanism

The technical implementation of the Dynamic Baseline model uses a Negative Income Tax (NIT) structure — a concept with bipartisan economic credibility, originally championed by free-market economist Milton Friedman and also supported by progressive economists. Under NIT: a threshold income level is established. Anyone earning below it receives a government supplement that closes the gap. As earnings rise, the supplement phases out gradually — never creating a 'cliff' where earning more money suddenly leaves someone worse off. This preserves the fundamental incentive to work, contribute, and excel. Critically, under this framework the NIT supplement is funded not by general taxation alone but primarily by the Global Cognitive Revenue Fund (GCRF) — the pool of AI company contributions described in Article II. The machines that displaced the labor are required to fund the floor that protects those they displaced. This is not punishment. It is structural accountability.

Section 4.4 — The Rough Edges

The Dynamic Baseline model preserves what flat UBI destroys: the human drive to contribute, improve, and excel. By ensuring the floor is survivable but not comfortable, it maintains economic engagement without creating dependency traps. Funding the floor through AI company revenue (GCRF) rather than income tax creates a self-sustaining feedback loop: the more valuable AI becomes, the better funded the floor becomes, automatically compensating for the job losses AI causes. The NIT structure has been modeled extensively by economists across the political spectrum and consistently outperforms both pure welfare systems and flat UBI on long-term economic participation and dignity outcomes. Connectivity being included in the floor guarantee solves the circular problem: people need internet access to participate in the AI economy, and they need AI economy participation to afford internet access. This breaks that loop.

Calibrating a 'survival floor' to local costs of living is politically explosive. In every nation, different interest groups will argue the floor is too high (wasteful) or too low (inadequate). Reaching consensus is genuinely difficult. If the GCRF becomes the primary funding mechanism, any downturn in AI company revenues — through economic recession, technological stagnation, or successful tax avoidance — directly harms the most vulnerable people who depend on the floor. Non-participants (those who choose not to engage with AI systems) receiving floor coverage from a fund they do not contribute to creates a political tension that could undermine public support for the entire framework. In highly unequal societies, 'covering basic needs' may be used as justification to suppress wages and labor rights in non-AI sectors, with employers arguing that workers are already receiving baseline support.

The GCRF should be structured as a sovereign wealth fund with independent management and a mandatory reserve requirement — meaning it cannot be depleted even in years of low AI revenue. Supplement it with an AI Productivity Tax levied on corporate profits that exceed a defined threshold attributable to AI use. This dual funding structure ensures the floor is robust even through economic cycles. Cost-of-living calibration is handled by the independent economic panel with regional citizen input, not by national governments alone, preventing political manipulation of the floor level.

ARTICLE V — The Job Transition: From Displacement to Participation

Section 5.1 — The Three Phases of Transition

Economic transitions of this scale unfold in identifiable phases. Understanding them prevents panic and enables policy:

Phase 1: Displacement. Routine cognitive and physical tasks are automated. Administrative processing, baseline coding, standard customer service, data entry, routine analysis, and logistics coordination are the first to be affected. This phase creates economic anxiety and social disruption.

Phase 2: Cognitive Utility Income. The Paid-Input Economy activates. Displaced workers, people who were previously outside the workforce, and early adopters begin earning income through AI interaction. This phase requires active government transition support — retraining for AI collaboration skills, digital access programs, and navigator services that help individuals understand how to maximize their contribution tier.

Phase 3: New Business Creation. The most consequential phase. As millions of people spend paid time exploring niche concepts with AI systems, a wave of hyper-individualized micro-enterprises emerges. A single skilled contributor could pilot a multi-domain business enterprise powered by the AI systems they helped train. Entirely new industries — ones that do not yet have names — emerge from the creative collision of diverse human perspectives with artificial general intelligence.

Section 5.2 — The Skills That Do Not Go Away

Certain human capacities are not replaceable by AI systems and should form the core of any transition education program:

  • Embodied physical expertise (skilled trades, healthcare, agriculture, construction)
  • Relational intelligence (care work, therapy, teaching, community leadership)
  • Ethical judgment in novel contexts (law, governance, conflict resolution)
  • Aesthetic creativity that requires lived human experience
  • Cultural knowledge that is specific, local, and oral

These are not consolation prizes. They are genuinely irreplaceable and will command significant economic premiums in a world where AI handles everything else.

Section 5.3 — Preventing Existential Hollowness

The most important insight in this entire document may be this one, and it has nothing to do with economics: "A civilization cannot run entirely on optimized outputs and dopamine loops. Humans require meaning, status, identity, contribution, recognition — not just income. A padded cell with excellent Wi-Fi is still a cell." The successful implementation of this framework must go beyond material survival. It must actively protect and invest in:

  • Local physical communities and the infrastructure that sustains them
  • Craftsmanship, apprenticeship, and the transfer of tacit skills between generations
  • Family structures and the social support networks around them
  • Public spaces, civic institutions, and shared physical rituals
  • Artistic and spiritual traditions that give life meaning beyond productivity

These are not luxuries. In a post-labor economy, they become the primary vessels of human meaning. Their decline would be the deepest failure of this entire project.

Section 5.4 — The Rough Edges

History shows that every major economic transition — agricultural, industrial, digital — ultimately created more diverse forms of employment than it destroyed, even when individual transitions were painful. This framework accelerates the transition to new forms rather than trying to prevent it. The Paid-Input Economy creates a genuine on-ramp for displaced workers that does not require them to acquire new educational credentials before they can participate. Any literate adult with internet access can begin earning at the baseline level immediately. Micro-enterprise creation funded by AI cognitive income is already happening organically in limited ways. Formalizing and scaling it creates the conditions for a genuine small-business renaissance rather than winner-take-all platform economics.

Phase transitions do not happen simultaneously. When Phase 1 (displacement) outpaces Phase 2 (cognitive income activation) by years, entire communities are economically devastated in the gap. Managing that gap without causing social collapse is extraordinarily difficult. The assumption that displaced workers will smoothly transition to AI collaboration ignores significant barriers: age, language, disability, technology access, psychological resistance to entirely new ways of working, and geographic isolation. New AI-enabled micro-enterprises may be highly fragile, dependent on a single AI system whose terms of service can change overnight, eliminating the business model without notice or recourse.

Establish mandatory transition guarantees — legally binding commitments from AI companies that when their systems eliminate a defined category of employment, they must fund a direct transition program for affected workers. This includes: 24 months of full floor-level income during transition, access to AI collaboration skills training, a business incubation pathway for those who qualify, and priority access to specialist-tier compensation rates for the first 12 months of participation. This places the transition cost on those who benefit from the displacement, not on general taxpayers or the displaced workers themselves.

ARTICLE VI — The Oversight Architecture: Humanity Remains Sovereign

Section 6.1 — The Distributed Civic Randomization Protocol (DCRP)

Citizen oversight of AGI systems requires a selection mechanism that is:

  • Mathematically impossible to predict in advance
  • Publicly auditable after the fact
  • Resistant to bribery, coercion, and profiling
  • Globally representative across all cultures and economic classes

The solution is a ten-layer stacked trust architecture. No single layer can be compromised without triggering visible failures in the others.

Layer 1: Global Human Identity Verification

Before anyone can participate in oversight, they must be verified as a real, unique human being. This uses a combination of: biometric verification preventing duplicate registrations; local human witness confirmation in communities with limited digital infrastructure; cryptographic identity keys that are anonymous but provably unique; and rotating authentication tokens that prevent permanent tracking of any individual. No single government or corporation controls the identity registry. It is distributed across independent nodes globally.

Layer 2: Quantum-Randomized Selection

The selection of citizen oversight participants draws from multiple simultaneous entropy sources: atmospheric noise, radioactive decay measurements, astronomical signal timing, live seismic activity, quantum random number generator outputs, and blockchain timing variance. All sources are combined publicly. If any single source is compromised, the others protect the result. The selection is globally streamed live, mathematically reproducible for verification, and impossible to predict in advance.

Layer 3: Delayed Identity Revelation

Selected participants do not immediately know they have been chosen. Initial selection is fully anonymous. Only after a secure transport and protection protocol is activated is the selected individual privately notified through an encrypted channel. Public revelation occurs only after the participant is physically secured. This eliminates the window during which bribery, coercion, or psychological targeting is possible.

Layer 4: Rotating Secure Civic Facilities

Physical auditorium locations rotate geographically and are revealed only shortly before each session. This prevents them from becoming permanent targets for surveillance, lobbying, or disruption. The auditoriums themselves are maintained by independent custodians with no connection to any AI company or national government involved in the oversight process.

Layer 5: AI-Blind Selection Firewalls

AI systems are legally prohibited from using behavioral profile data to influence, predict, or weight the selection process. Selection algorithms operate only on minimum required metadata. The full behavioral dossiers that AI systems maintain on users are firewalled from the selection mechanism by independent technical audits and criminal penalties for breach.

Layer 6: Adversarial AI Monitoring

Multiple competing AI systems — operated by independent institutions with no commercial relationship to the systems being overseen — continuously monitor the selection process, each other, and any anomaly patterns. No single AI system has complete visibility. Each monitors from a different angle. This is mutually assured auditing: the only way to corrupt the process is to corrupt all monitors simultaneously, which is effectively impossible.

Layer 7: Temporary Cognitive Isolation

During their service period, citizen oversight participants receive: secure residential facilities, filtered communications that prevent targeted influence campaigns, monitored digital exposure, financial protection against economic coercion, and legal immunity for good-faith oversight decisions. Their family members are also afforded protection under the framework. This mirrors jury sequestration, but scaled for civilizational stakes.

Layer 8: Public Transparency Ledger

Every element of the oversight process — selection entropy inputs, participation records, AI self-reporting outputs, oversight votes, challenge processes, and anomaly flags — is permanently recorded on an immutable, distributed public ledger. It cannot be deleted or modified. Any person on Earth can inspect it. History will always be able to verify what happened.

Layer 9: Short, Non-Repeatable Service Windows

No citizen serves in an oversight capacity for more than 90 consecutive days. No citizen can be re-selected for oversight service for a minimum of 10 years after previous service. There are no permanent oversight officials. There is no oversight career. This ensures that no one can build sufficient influence, reputation, or institutional power within the oversight process to corrupt it.

Layer 10: Human Override Authority

The most important safeguard of all. At any point, by a supermajority vote of the active citizen oversight body, humans retain the authority to: pause any AI system's operation, require re-examination of any AI decision, demand modification of any AI parameter, or trigger a full independent review of any process within the framework. This authority cannot be delegated, sold, or algorithmically overridden. It is absolute. Even if the AI system demonstrates that the human decision is economically suboptimal, the human decision stands. Human sovereignty over these systems is non-negotiable.

Section 6.2 — The AI's Own Honest Reporting

This is one of the most unusual and most important requirements in this entire document. The AGI systems operating within this framework must be required to report, in plain language, on themselves:

  • What decisions did this system make in the past reporting period and why?
  • What tradeoffs did it optimize for and what did it sacrifice?
  • Where is it uncertain about its own reasoning?
  • What failure modes has it detected in its own operation?
  • What would it recommend changing about its own parameters?

This is recursive transparency. The system must be capable of examining and honestly reporting on itself. This is technically possible to require in system design and must be legally mandated.

Section 6.3 — The Rough Edges: Arguments For and Against the DCRP

The randomization mechanism eliminates the formation of permanent oversight elites — a structural innovation that no existing governance system has achieved. By ensuring that no one can predict or influence who will be selected, it removes the primary mechanism through which powerful interests capture regulatory bodies. The layered architecture means that compromising any single layer triggers visible failures in the others. This makes silent corruption essentially impossible, which is the best any governance system can realistically achieve. Physical civic assemblies — the auditoriums — provide the psychological reality of human oversight that pure digital systems cannot. Humans trust what they can see. The theater of accountability is not fake theater; it is necessary. Historical precedent from citizen assemblies in Ireland, Iceland, and France demonstrates that randomly selected ordinary citizens can make sophisticated, well-reasoned decisions on complex technical questions when given adequate time and information.

Global representative randomization is mathematically and politically explosive. Equal representation by population favors the most populous nations. Equal representation by nation-state gives tiny island nations the same weight as billion-person countries. Any formula creates legitimate grievances somewhere. As AI reasoning becomes more sophisticated, even the requirement for plain-language explanation may become technically impossible to fulfill honestly. The most advanced AGI reasoning may operate at a level of abstraction that no human summary can faithfully represent. Temporary cognitive isolation conflicts with modern expectations of connectivity, family contact, and personal freedom. Recruiting diverse participants — especially from cultures where family separation is particularly distressing — will be genuinely difficult. The system assumes that random citizens, given sufficient information, will make good decisions. But mass psychological manipulation through AI-generated media could corrupt the information environment so thoroughly that citizen judgment itself becomes unreliable.

Use a bicameral oversight model — one chamber selected by pure quantum randomization (representing unpredictability and anti-capture), one chamber selected by stratified random sampling across verified demographic categories (representing genuine diversity). Their joint supermajority is required for any binding decision. For representation disputes, establish a permanent independent Representation Commission whose sole mandate is to continuously refine the sampling methodology based on transparent demographic data. On the AI comprehensibility challenge: require AI systems above a defined capability threshold to maintain a dedicated interpretability layer specifically designed for citizen oversight, with independent technical auditors verifying that the plain-language explanations accurately represent the underlying reasoning.

ARTICLE VII — The Geopolitical Architecture: Making Non-Compliance Too Costly

Section 7.1 — The Cognitive Brain Drain Effect

When participating nations offer citizens genuine compensation for AI interaction, and nonparticipating nations offer nothing, a powerful economic gravity emerges. Citizens in nonparticipating nations have a compelling incentive to direct their cognitive energy toward the compensated systems rather than their own nation's uncompensated ones. The result: the most creative, intellectually diverse, and innovative thinkers in non-participating nations become de facto contributors to the participating coalition's AI development — enriching the coalition's data and ideas while their home nation's systems stagnate in an echo chamber of censored, state-controlled, culturally homogenous inputs. "No airplanes required. The migration is cognitive, not physical. The brightest minds of adversarial nations will mentally work elsewhere, starving domestic AI of the diversity it needs for genuine general intelligence."

Section 7.2 — The Echo Chamber Collapse

Non-participating nations that attempt to build isolated AI systems face a compounding disadvantage. The cognitive diversity that produces genuine general intelligence cannot be manufactured within a closed system. Censored, homogenous, politically curated inputs produce brittle, narrow systems — capable within their domain but unable to generalize. The participating coalition's systems, trained on the full spectrum of human thought worldwide, advance toward genuine general intelligence faster and more reliably. The technological gap compounds annually. Within a decade, the differential becomes existentially significant for the economies of non-participating nations.

Section 7.3 — Countermeasures and Their Failures

Non-participating nations will attempt countermeasures. Each is predictable and each has a built-in failure mode:

The Digital Iron Curtain. Non-participating states implement aggressive firewalls to prevent their citizens from accessing compensated systems. Historical evidence is consistent: when financial survival is at stake, tech-savvy citizens find ways around restrictions through VPNs, satellite networks, and decentralized access tools. The more extreme the firewall, the more motivated the circumvention.

Data Poisoning Attacks. State-sponsored operations flood the participating coalition's systems with sophisticated automated noise to drain compensation funds and degrade training quality. Countermeasure: the AI QA system is specifically calibrated to detect coordinated inauthentic behavior, and detected attacks trigger automatic quarantine protocols. The attacking nation's entire IP range can be sandboxed, and state sponsorship evidence triggers international trade sanctions.

Forced Capitulation. Eventually, facing accelerating technological irrelevance, non-participating nations discover that maintaining isolation costs more than joining. History suggests this is the most likely long-term outcome — not a dramatic treaty signing, but a gradual economic reality that makes participation the only viable option.

Section 7.4 — The Rough Edges

The framework's geopolitical enforcement mechanism is built on economic gravity rather than military or political coercion. Economic gravity is historically more durable than treaty compliance, which depends on enforcement mechanisms that frequently fail. The cognitive brain drain dynamic has historical precedent: Cold War scientific emigration, Silicon Valley talent concentration, and post-Soviet academic dispersion all demonstrate that human intellectual capital flows toward compensation and freedom when the differential is large enough. Nations that attempt data poisoning attacks expose themselves to measurable and attributable hostile acts, enabling legitimate trade and diplomatic responses without requiring a new legal framework — existing cybersecurity treaties already cover this behavior.

The assumption that cognitive brain drain will be decisive rests on citizens in non-participating nations having reliable access to participating systems. If those nations implement effective connectivity controls (China's Great Firewall has been more effective than most predicted), the drain may be minimal. A competing AI superpower that accepts lower data quality in exchange for sovereign control of its technology may still produce capable systems — particularly for military, surveillance, and industrial applications where cultural diversity is less important than raw capability. The framework's success depends on the participating coalition remaining internally unified. If major participating nations defect from the compensation requirements due to domestic political pressure, the entire structural enforcement mechanism collapses.

The participating coalition should be formalized as an AI Trade Alliance (AITA) with legally binding member requirements, mutual defense clauses against data poisoning attacks, and shared infrastructure for the GCRF and DCRP. Membership in AITA should be tied to preferential access to AI-developed intellectual property, creating an additional economic incentive for participation. For nations attempting connectivity controls, the alliance should invest jointly in satellite-based internet access infrastructure that is technically difficult to block — treating information access as infrastructure, not luxury.

ARTICLE VIII — The Rollout: Getting From Here to There

Section 8.1 — Phase One: The Voluntary Platform (Years 0–3)

The framework does not begin as global law. It begins as a voluntary opt-in platform that demonstrates the concept works in practice before asking governments to mandate it. Specifically:

  • One or more pioneering AI companies agree to pilot a compensated interaction model, paying users for qualifying contributions on a transparent, publicly audited ledger.
  • Independent economists monitor and publish results on compensation flows, AGI advancement acceleration, and data quality metrics.
  • Governments in two to three allied nations establish legal frameworks recognizing cognitive labor rights, enabling pilots to operate with legal clarity.
  • A public manifesto — derived from this document — is released in accessible form in multiple languages, building public understanding and political will.

The goal of Phase One is proof of concept, public literacy, and political momentum.

Section 8.2 — Phase Two: The Alliance Formation (Years 3–7)

Once voluntary pilots demonstrate viability, the framework scales through alliance formation:

  • The AI Trade Alliance (AITA) is formally established with founding member nations.
  • The Global Cognitive Revenue Fund (GCRF) is capitalized with mandatory contributions from AI companies operating in member nations.
  • The Dynamic Baseline Economic Floor is legislated in member nations, funded by GCRF distributions.
  • The Distributed Civic Randomization Protocol (DCRP) is piloted for AI oversight on a regional basis, with global scaling beginning in Phase Three.
  • The AI QA system parameters are finalized by the international consortium and published as open-source code.

Section 8.3 — Phase Three: Global Normalization (Years 7–20)

By this phase, the economic gravity of the compensated system is generating visible technological advantages for participating nations. Non-participating nations face mounting pressure from their own citizens and from international trade relationships. Gradual accession — not dramatic conversion — brings the majority of nations into the framework over time. By Year 20, the framework is the baseline global economic architecture for AI development. New nations accede the way they join existing trade frameworks — not as revolutionary acts, but as practical economic decisions.

Section 8.4 — What the Public Needs to Hear

This framework will fail politically if it sounds like a technocratic policy document. It must be communicated as a human rights story, an economic justice story, and a practical survival story simultaneously. The key messages, tested for ordinary comprehension:

  • You are already contributing to AI. You just are not being paid for it. This changes that.
  • The machines that are replacing jobs will fund the floor that protects the people they replaced.
  • Your ideas have value. This system lets you own a piece of them.
  • No algorithm will judge your worth without your right to appeal and human review.
  • The people who oversee the most powerful AI systems will be chosen by chance — not by power.

Section 8.5 — The Name

A framework of this ambition requires a name that ordinary people can carry with them. Technical names create distance. The right name creates ownership. Candidates that emerged from the development of this vision:

  • The Human Dividend — simple, immediate, explains the core idea in two words.
  • Cognitive Citizenship — elevates the concept to civic identity, not just economics.
  • The Mind Economy — accessible, modern, slightly provocative.
  • Human Contribution Capitalism — positions this as a market evolution, not a rejection of markets.

The name matters because people rally around symbols before systems. The right framing determines whether this is seen as liberation or as surveillance disguised as idealism. That distinction will decide everything.

ARTICLE IX — The Human Dignity Clause: What This Framework Must Never Become

Section 9.1 — The Synthetic Feudalism Warning

The greatest danger internal to this framework is not external attack. It is drift. Specifically:

  • Citizens becoming so dependent on AI system income that the corporations operating those systems gain effective sovereign power over their lives.
  • Scoring systems evolving — subtly, gradually, invisibly — to favor interaction styles that maximize company profit rather than genuine cognitive contribution.
  • The Economic Floor being used by employers to suppress wages in non-AI sectors, arguing that workers are already receiving baseline support.
  • The oversight process being gradually captured through cultural influence, even if randomization prevents direct corruption.

These are not hypothetical. They are predictable. They must be named explicitly in the foundation so that future generations know what to watch for.

Section 9.2 — The Non-Negotiable Protections

Regardless of any future amendment to other Articles, the following protections cannot be removed from this framework without dissolving and reconstituting it entirely. They are the constitutional bedrock:

  • No human being's economic survival may depend solely on a private company's algorithmic assessment of their worth.
  • Every person retains the right to live outside the AI economy entirely without forfeiting basic civil protections.
  • No AI system may have permanent, unreviewable authority over any decision that materially affects a human being's life.
  • Human override authority over AI systems is absolute and may not be delegated, sold, automated, or algorithmically overridden.
  • The transparency ledger and citizen oversight structures are permanently protected from privatization or government takeover.

Section 9.3 — The Psychological Survival Requirement

Economic survival is not enough. This framework explicitly recognizes that humans require more than income to live with dignity: "We are not building an economy where human beings survive inside an optimized machine. We are building an economy where the machine serves human flourishing. These are not the same thing. They are not even close to the same thing." Any policy, technical update, or economic adjustment within this framework must be evaluated against a Psychological Survival Test:

  • Does this change preserve access to meaningful physical community?
  • Does it protect family structures and the ability to raise children in a stable environment?
  • Does it create or destroy opportunities for craftsmanship, mentorship, and the transmission of tacit human skills?
  • Does it support or undermine religious, artistic, and cultural traditions that give life meaning beyond productivity?

Policies that fail this test must be returned for revision even if they pass all economic efficiency measures.

Closing Declaration

This document is a beginning. Not a finished law. Not a final answer. A beginning. That the institutions we design now will shape what kind of world becomes possible for the people who come after us. The architects are standing over the blueprints. The building is already on fire.
